Why Gen3?

Gen2 uses American Airlines’ proprietary Snowflake database, which cannot be publicly reproduced — a major limitation for academic papers. Gen3’s goal is:

Achieve reproducible and auditable results on fully public data using the same methodological framework.

12-Rule Leakage Control

Gen3 designed explicit availability audit rules for every feature, ensuring all features are obtainable at T-2h to T-6h before departure:

Tip

34/34 features passed the 12-Rule Leakage Checklist ✅

Schedule features: Known hours/days before departure, all safe Airport static: Long-term stable data, safe Calendar features: Astronomical facts, safe METAR weather: merge_asof(backward) ensures only observations with timestamp ≤ cutoff time TAF forecasts: Uses forecast time ≤ T-2h with validity period covering arrival time Tail chain features: Uses actual arrival time to determine if previous flight is known (same V9c fix)

Core Finding

Tip

“You can tell if a flight will be delayed just by looking at the schedule”

Under strict leakage control, schedule-derived features (dep_hour, dep_month, is_holiday_period, etc.) systematically dominate predictions. More than half of the predictable portion of delays is determined the moment the schedule is set.

What This Means

Delays are not random accidents — they are structural problems. Certain time slots (morning vs evening), certain months (around holidays), and certain routes have inherently higher delay risk built into the schedule.

Gen2 ablation data supports this conclusion: - Schedule-only baseline: AUC = 0.6226 - Full features (43): AUC = 0.7279 - Schedule features explain ~56% of predictable variance (0.6226 / 0.7279)

Gap in the Literature

No paper has ever quantified “what proportion is schedule-dominated and why” using SHAP under strict leakage control.

FDPP-ML (Journal of Big Data, 2023) noted that delays can be predicted using only schedule features, but did not explain why or quantify the proportion.

We rigorously validate this at 19.15M flight scale — this is not a geographic anomaly but a systematic structural finding.

Implications for the Aviation Industry

If delays are primarily determined by schedule structure, then: - Flight scheduling optimization is more effective than real-time intervention - Overcrowded scheduling during high-delay-probability periods is the fundamental problem - The core value of “advance prediction” is enabling passengers and operators to prepare early, not “learning about delays only when they happen”

Core Finding

Tip

Under strict pre-departure leakage control, weather features’ SHAP contribution in Gen3 is near zero (relative to tail chain and schedule).

This contradicts most literature conclusions — but we believe this is a methodological difference, not a real-world difference.

Why Weather is Unimportant in Our Model

Physical reasons: - METAR weather observations 2–6 hours before departure represent “current” weather, not weather at arrival time - Long-haul flights (e.g., DFW → JFK, 3–4 hour flight time) have limited destination weather prediction accuracy at departure time - TAF forecast temporal resolution (typical forecast intervals of 1–6 hours) means forecasts still differ from actual arrival weather

Methodological reasons (most critical):

Warning

Why weather appears “important” in existing literature

Many papers compute weather features using post-departure or post-arrival weather observations (actual departure time, METAR at arrival), constituting information leakage. Under strict T-2h cutoff conditions, these “future weather” values are unavailable, and weather’s apparent importance drops dramatically.

Tip

Our contribution: The US has complex and variable weather. Weather being “still unimportant” can only be explained by methodology — under strict pre-departure leakage control, weather’s apparent importance may be primarily an artifact of measurement bias.

No paper has ever presented this finding in a strict leakage-controlled experiment at US continental scale.
